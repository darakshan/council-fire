Rudolf Steiner (27 February 1861 – 30 March 1925) was an Austrian philosopher, esotericist, social reformer, architect, and prolific lecturer who founded anthroposophy—a spiritual-philosophical movement he described as “spiritual science.”

Life Overview

Born Rudolf Joseph Lorenz Steiner in what is now Donji Kraljevec, Croatia (then part of the Austrian Empire), he grew up in a German-speaking family amid the Alps and later near Vienna. As a child he reported early spiritual experiences, including seeing the spirit of a deceased relative. He studied mathematics, physics, chemistry, and philosophy at the Vienna Institute of Technology (1879–1883) and earned a PhD in philosophy from the University of Rostock in 1891 with a thesis on Fichte.

In the 1890s he worked in Weimar editing Goethe’s scientific writings, which profoundly shaped his holistic view of nature and knowledge. He moved to Berlin in 1897, edited literary magazines, lectured at workers’ colleges, and wrote his seminal philosophical work The Philosophy of Freedom (1894, also known as The Philosophy of Spiritual Activity), arguing for ethical individualism and a path to spiritual knowledge beyond sensory perception.

Founding Anthroposophy

Around 1900 Steiner became involved with the Theosophical Society and led its German section from 1902. After major disagreements (especially over Annie Besant’s promotion of Jiddu Krishnamurti as a “world teacher”), he and his followers split in 1912–1913 to form the independent Anthroposophical Society. Steiner redefined theosophical ideas with a strongly Western, Christian-Gnostic, and Rosicrucian emphasis, replacing Eastern terminology and focusing on human freedom, reincarnation, and the evolution of consciousness.

He delivered over 6,000 lectures and wrote dozens of books, including core texts such as:

  • How to Know Higher Worlds (1904–1905)
  • Theosophy (1904)
  • An Outline of Esoteric Science (1910)

Practical Contributions

Steiner’s ideas were never purely theoretical. He applied them across many fields:

  • Education — In 1919 he helped found the first Waldorf School (Waldorf-Astoria cigarette factory school in Stuttgart). Waldorf education emphasizes artistic, imaginative, and rhythmic development in three seven-year stages and is now practiced in over 1,000 schools worldwide.
  • Agriculture — In 1924 he gave the “Agriculture Course” that launched biodynamic agriculture—the first modern organic farming movement—treating the farm as a living organism and using specially prepared herbal and mineral sprays aligned with lunar and planetary rhythms.
  • Medicine — With Dr. Ita Wegman he developed anthroposophical medicine (integrating conventional treatment with spiritual insights); the pharmaceutical company Weleda grew out of this work.
  • Arts & Architecture — He created eurythmy (a movement art expressing speech and music) and wrote four “mystery dramas.” He designed the Goetheanum in Dornach, Switzerland—first a wooden building destroyed by fire in 1922, then the iconic concrete Second Goetheanum (completed after his death), now a UNESCO-listed cultural site and headquarters of the Anthroposophical Society.
  • Social Reform — After World War I he proposed the “Threefold Social Order” (independent spheres of culture, politics, and economy) as an alternative to both capitalism and socialism.

Legacy and Reception

The Anthroposophical Society and its initiatives continue today with branches worldwide. Steiner’s work has influenced New Age thought, holistic education, organic farming, and alternative medicine. Supporters see him as a profound visionary who expanded human knowledge into spiritual realms; critics regard many of his claims (Atlantis/Lemuria, clairvoyance, certain racial and cosmological ideas) as pseudoscience or pseudohistory. He has also faced accusations of racism or antisemitism in some esoteric passages (though these are contextualized by scholars within his broader emphasis on individual spiritual development).

Steiner died in Dornach, Switzerland, at age 64 after a period of declining health. His complete works (Gesamtausgabe) fill over 350 volumes.
