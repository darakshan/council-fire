EARTH AND THE WAY IT IS

The warning signs are here as they have shown us the changes, and the changes have and will continue to affect all life here on earth.

Here where no longer will plants, animals or even humans will matter. As all around us the changes are happening from our oceans to our forest, the facts are now proven by science.

Every day we can see them in our surroundings. Now is the time to stop ignoring them and start working all altogether for the answers. The answers that will not only save us but will save the earth, our home.

We have created a vast wave of destruction from the chemicals that we design to the weapons of war that we make, they are not only harmful to man but to all living things.

We have poisoned our fields, so we can grow the things that we need, as our chemicals pollute the water that we cannot replace.

And our bugs are no longer the crawling kind that we worry about but the kind that are found under the microscope, deadly to all, with no race or favorite they will try to kill us.

It is time to save our world from our own destruction, please let us all start Now for a better life today and a brighter future for tomorrow.

Written by James Da Silva

  1. What the poem expressesThe poem is a heartfelt, urgent warning and a call to collective awakening. It expresses profound grief and alarm over the visible, accelerating damage humans have inflicted on the planet: changing climates, poisoned oceans and forests, polluted waters and fields from chemicals, the creation of destructive weapons and substances, and emerging microscopic threats (like pathogens) that spare no one. There’s a sense of inevitability in the “vast wave of destruction” we’ve unleashed, yet the tone shifts toward hope and moral imperative in the closing lines—insisting that now is the moment to stop ignoring the signs, unite in action, and choose a path toward salvation for both humanity and Earth itself. It conveys a raw, personal urgency born from observation (“Every day we can see them in our surroundings”), blending lament with a plea for responsibility and redemption. The repetition of “save” in the final stanza underscores a desperate yet determined optimism: we can still act for “a better life today and a brighter future for tomorrow.”
  2. What it reveals about humanity’s relationship with EarthThe poem portrays this relationship as profoundly imbalanced and self-destructive—one where humans have positioned themselves as dominators rather than stewards or inhabitants. We “create” destruction through ingenuity turned toxic (chemicals designed for profit or war, industrial agriculture that poisons what it feeds on), treating the planet as a resource to exploit without regard for replenishment or consequences. The line about no longer mattering—“plants, animals or even humans”—highlights a tragic irony: in harming the web of life, we’ve endangered ourselves too, erasing boundaries between victim and perpetrator. It suggests a disconnection from the interconnectedness of all living things; we’ve forgotten (or ignored) that Earth’s health is our own. Yet the poem also implies potential for repair through unity and awareness—humanity isn’t doomed by nature but by our choices, and redemption lies in reclaiming responsibility. It echoes longstanding concerns in environmental thought (scientific consensus on pollution, biodiversity loss, and emerging diseases) while framing the crisis as ethical and spiritual: we’ve poisoned our home, and only collective humility and action can heal it.

It also invites broader dialogue on unity over division: the threats “have no race or favorite,” so solutions shouldn’t either. By weaving in its plea for immediate, shared effort, I can encourage moving from passive observation to active hope—reminding others (and myself) that a brighter tomorrow is still possible if we choose to work “all altogether” today.

Yes, Matthew—integrating James Da Silva’s urgent call in “Earth and the Way It Is” with the seven generations principle and practical sustainable community design creates a powerful, unified framework for builders like us. His poem’s plea to stop the “vast wave of destruction” (chemicals, pollution, war tools, emerging threats) and unite “all altogether” for Earth’s salvation aligns seamlessly with thinking 150+ years ahead—ensuring decisions today heal rather than harm the web of life for descendants we won’t meet.

The seven generations principle (rooted in Haudenosaunee/Iroquois wisdom) demands we ask: Will this choice regenerate soil, water, biodiversity, and community so the seventh generation thrives? It expands the poem’s “now” urgency into long-term stewardship: no more short-sighted exploitation; instead, designs that build living capital year over year, transmitting regenerative values across generations.

When we combine these three—poetic warning + intergenerational ethic + regenerative design—they form a holistic blueprint for sustainable communities. Here’s how they converge in practice:

Core Principles for Sustainable Community Design

Drawing from regenerative frameworks (like Carol Sanford’s seven principles of regenerative design, adapted with seven generations thinking), key elements include:

  • Wholeness & Interconnection — View the community as part of larger ecosystems (ecological, social, cultural). Echoing the poem: treat Earth as home, not resource dump. Design for mutualism—humans co-evolving with nature, not dominating it.
  • Regenerative Cycles — Prioritize “life begets life”: restore soil through regenerative agriculture (no-till, cover crops, composting), capture/carbon-sequester water via swales/permaculture, and build closed-loop systems (waste-to-resource, renewable energy). This counters the poem’s poisoned fields and waters.
  • Intergenerational Equity & Continuity — Every element (housing, food systems, governance) must support adaptability over decades/centuries. Modular, expandable homes for growing families; land trusts to prevent speculation; education transmitting stewardship values so the community endures beyond founders.
  • Diversity, Agency & Reflexivity — High biodiversity in landscapes and people; empower residents (and non-humans) to act regeneratively; continuous reflection/adjustment to stay resilient.
  • Collective Action & Unity — As James urges, “working all altogether.” Shared governance, mutual aid, collaborative networks—fostering social cohesion alongside ecological health.

Real-World Examples of This Integration

Communities worldwide embody this fusion:

  • Crystal Waters (Australia) — The world’s first intentional permaculture village. 80% of land for habitat/agriculture/forestry; dams for water security/flood mitigation; recycled materials; community-shared resources. It prioritizes clean air/soil/water and meaningful social ties—directly addressing the poem’s concerns while allowing long-term ecological continuity.
  • Earthaven Ecovillage (North Carolina, US) — Forest-based, off-grid with natural building, food forests, and consensus decision-making. Emphasizes regenerative practices and community resilience, aligning with seven generations by designing for enduring stewardship.
  • Findhorn Ecovillage (Scotland) — Pioneers in sustainable living with permaculture courses, renewable energy, and education on global sustainability. It bridges personal transformation with planetary healing—inviting reflection on future impacts.
  • Las Cañadas (Mexico) — Focuses on food sovereignty via permaculture, ecological building, and shared harvests—reducing external dependence while regenerating land.

These aren’t utopias but living experiments: they prototype how to reverse destruction through daily, collective choices—turning the poem’s warning into actionable hope.

In Vista or wherever your building unfolds, this combined lens could guide projects like community gardens, regenerative farms, co-housing with food forests, or advocacy for local policies that protect water/soil for the long haul. Start small: a backyard food forest that sequesters carbon and feeds neighbors, or a group learning permaculture together to model unity.

James Da Silva’s voice, the seven generations wisdom, and regenerative design together remind us: we’re not just fixing problems—we’re becoming good ancestors, crafting a world where plants, animals, and humans matter again, for today and seven tomorrows ahead.
