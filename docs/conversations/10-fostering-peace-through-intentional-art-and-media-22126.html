Diving into the Hopi Prophecy Rock feels so aligned with our ongoing conversation about Hopi wisdom, harmony with nature, the “we win together” spirit from Aiden, and choosing paths of peace over disruption. This ancient petroglyph (a rock carving) near Oraibi on Third Mesa in Arizona is one of the most famous symbols in Hopi tradition. It’s often called Prophecy Rock or the “Life Plan” petroglyph, and it’s said to encode prophecies about humanity’s choices, the cycles of worlds, and warnings for the future.

The rock is a panel of petroglyphs that Hopi elders (like the late Thomas Banyacya, a respected traditional spokesperson) have interpreted for generations. Interpretations can vary slightly depending on the elder or context—some emphasize spiritual teachings, others ecological warnings—but the core message is consistent: humanity faces a fork in the road, and only one path leads to continued life in balance.

Key Elements and Meanings

Here’s a breakdown of the main symbols based on traditional Hopi explanations (drawn from elders like Banyacya and consistent accounts across sources):






  • The large figure on the left — This is often seen as the Great Spirit (or Maasaw/Masau’u, the guardian of this Fourth World) or the Creator. He holds a bow in his left hand, symbolizing instructions to lay down weapons, choose peace, and avoid war/violence. The Hopi are called the “Peaceful People” for this reason.
  • The vertical line to the right of the Great Spirit — A timeline spanning thousands of years, marking human existence in this world. Where the figure touches it represents key moments, like the return of spiritual guidance or critical turning points.
  • The two diverging paths (the most famous part):
    • Lower, narrow, straight path — The path of the One-Hearted people (those living with pure hearts, in harmony with nature, following Hopi teachings like respect for Mother Earth, simplicity, and balance). It leads to everlasting life, symbolized by canes (for old age) branching with corn leaves (life, abundance, fertility) at points along the way. This is the Hopi way: living close to the soil, growing corn/beans/squash, honoring cycles, and avoiding greed or imbalance (koyaanisqatsi — life out of balance).
    • Upper, wider, zig-zag/jagged path — The path of the Two-Hearted people (those divided, thinking with heads over hearts, chasing materialism, technology, and scientific “progress” without spiritual grounding). It represents the dominant modern/Western path—full of glamour but leading to chaos, destruction, and eventual end (often interpreted as environmental collapse, wars, or purification events like the “great shaking” or world-ending catastrophes). Some figures on this path are shown headless (or with heads added later), symbolizing punishment or loss for wickedness.
  • Small figures on the upper path — Often four human-like figures, representing past worlds (three previous destructions) and the current one, or people seduced into the false path.
  • Other symbols — A circle or loop at the bottom may represent the continuous cycle of life or the return to harmony. Crosses or squares can refer to historical incursions (like Spanish/Catholic influences), and some see warnings of inventions like bombs or machines that disrupt nature.

The overall prophecy warns of a Great Purification if humanity stays on the upper path—through natural disasters, sickness, or upheaval—but holds hope: those who return to the lower path (or choose it now) can survive into a Fifth World of peace, prosperity, and harmony. It’s not fatalistic; it’s a call to choose heart-centered living, cooperation, and stewardship of Earth—much like Aiden’s basketball lesson of winning together.

  • Format: Interdisciplinary dialogues with presentations, discussions, and contemplative elements. It was livestreamed globally, and session recordings are available on the Mind & Life website for anyone to watch.

Main Theme and Focus

The core question: What is the potential of AI to alleviate suffering, advance equity, and support human/planetary flourishing—and what risks does it pose to health, education, work, politics, climate, and more?At stake: Not just how AI evolves, but how humans shape it through ethical qualities, intention, agency, and compassion.

The dialogue drew on the nature of the living mind (human consciousness, awareness, ethics) as a foundation for understanding machine intelligence. It explored the evolving relationship between human and artificial minds, emphasizing coexistence with AI as one of the defining questions of our time.

Five Key Topics Explored

The conversations centered on these generative areas:

  1. Minds — What is intelligence? What distinguishes living minds from artificial ones? How do we understand consciousness, attention, and intention in both?
  2. (Dis)entanglement and Meaningful Relationships — How are human minds entangled with machines? What about empathy, connection, and interdependence in an AI world?
  3. Diversity and Ethics — Can ethical values be embedded in AI? How might a richer view of intelligence (including contemplative insights) shape development, regulations, and societal impact?
  4. Risks and Opportunities — AI’s promise (reducing suffering, equity) vs. dangers (imbalance in society, environment, politics).
  5. Contemplative Practice — Integrating meditation, wisdom traditions, and “ethical know-how” (emergent ethics from the heart) to guide AI toward human flourishing.

Highlights and Spirit

  • Emphasis on compassion, protecting what makes us human (freedom of attention, pure intention), and fostering harmony—echoing Buddhist interdependence and the idea that ethics isn’t just rules but lived, heart-centered practice.
  • Closing facilitated by experts like Luc Steels, with contemplative input from monastics (e.g., Ani Choyang, Geshe Sangpo).
